Why AI Literacy Belongs in High School

Students are already encountering AI in school, at home, online, and in the tools they use every day. The question is no longer whether students will use AI. The question is whether they will learn to use it honestly, safely, and thoughtfully.

AI literacy helps students understand what AI can do, where it can mislead them, how to verify information, how to protect privacy, and how to use technology in ways that strengthen learning instead of replacing it.

AI should help students think better, not think less.

Featured Curriculum: AI Literacy Starter Kit

The AI Literacy Starter Kit is a classroom-ready introduction to responsible artificial intelligence use for middle and high school students. It is based on Ron Aceto's student guide, AI for Students: A Practical Guide for School, Learning, and Life, and is designed for schools that want practical AI instruction without turning the class into a coding camp.

The starter kit helps students understand what AI is, how AI tools generate responses, why AI can make mistakes, how to write better prompts, how to verify information, how to protect privacy, and how to use AI ethically in school.

These resources include Tennessee Computer Science Foundations standards connections where applicable. Final alignment should be confirmed against local district pacing, approved course placement, and teacher directions.

What Students Learn

Use AI to support learning, not replace thinking
Ask better questions and write stronger prompts
Check AI-generated information before trusting it
Protect personal information and respect privacy
Use AI as a study coach, brainstorming partner, and feedback tool
Recognize when AI use becomes dishonest or unhelpful
Apply AI responsibly across school subjects

Module B

AI + Coding Starter Kit

Helping students use AI as a coding coach, not a code replacement.

The AI + Coding Starter Kit helps students use AI responsibly while learning introductory programming. Students practice asking better coding questions, interpreting Python errors, debugging simple programs, testing revisions, and explaining their work in their own words.

This module is designed for Computer Science Foundations, introductory Python, AI literacy, and digital ethics instruction. It reinforces the idea that AI can explain, hint, debug, and generate practice, but students must still read code, make decisions, test results, document changes, and own the final work.

These resources may support Tennessee Department of Education Computer Science Foundations (C10H11) standards, including CSF 9.2, CSF 13.1, CSF 15.1, CSF 16.1, and CSF 16.2, depending on the resource and classroom use. Confirm final alignment against local district pacing, approved course placement, and teacher directions.

My Teaching Approach

My teaching approach combines real-world technology experience with practical classroom instruction. I focus on helping students build confidence, ask better questions, solve problems, and connect technology skills to future opportunities.

In AI literacy, computer science, and cybersecurity, students need more than tools. They need judgment. They need structure. They need practice. They need to understand when technology helps and when human thinking must stay in charge.
